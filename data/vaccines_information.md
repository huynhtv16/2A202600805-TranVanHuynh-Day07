# Vaccine Information & FAQ

## What are vaccines?

Vaccines are biological preparations that improve immunity against a particular disease. A vaccine typically contains an agent that resembles a disease-causing microorganism. It is often made from a weakened or killed form of the microbe, its toxins, or one of its surface proteins.

## How do vaccines work?

Vaccines work by training and preparing the body's natural defenses (the immune system) to recognize and fight specific diseases. When vaccinated, the immune system learns to identify harmful pathogens and create antibodies to fight them. If you're later exposed to the actual disease, your immune system is ready to protect you.

## Are vaccines safe?

Yes, vaccines are safe. Before any vaccine is approved for use, it goes through rigorous testing for safety and effectiveness. Vaccines are continuously monitored even after they are approved and in use. The benefits of vaccination far outweigh the risks of getting the disease.

## Common vaccine side effects

Most vaccine side effects are mild and temporary, including:
- Soreness, redness, or swelling at the injection site
- Low-grade fever
- Fatigue or headache
- Muscle aches

These side effects typically go away within a few days.

## Serious vaccine side effects

Serious side effects are very rare. Healthcare providers and vaccine safety monitoring systems continuously watch for any unusual patterns or serious side effects.

## Vaccination schedule for children

The CDC recommends vaccinations at specific ages to provide the best protection. The childhood immunization schedule includes:
- Vaccines for diphtheria, tetanus, and pertussis (DTaP)
- Vaccines for measles, mumps, and rubella (MMR)
- Vaccines for polio (IPV)
- Vaccines for hepatitis B and hepatitis A
- Vaccines for chickenpox (varicella)
- Vaccines for influenza (flu)

## Adult vaccination

Adults should stay up to date with vaccinations. Recommended vaccines for adults include:
- Annual influenza vaccine
- Tetanus booster (every 10 years)
- Pneumococcal vaccine (for those 65+)
- Shingles vaccine (for those 50+)

## Can vaccines cause autism?

No. Numerous scientific studies have found no link between vaccines and autism. The original study that raised this concern was fraudulent and has been retracted.

## Should I get vaccinated if I'm pregnant?

Some vaccines are safe during pregnancy, while others should be avoided. Pregnant individuals should discuss vaccination with their healthcare provider. The flu vaccine and the whooping cough (pertussis) vaccine are generally recommended during pregnancy.

## What if I have allergies?

Inform your healthcare provider about any allergies before vaccination. Most people with allergies can safely receive vaccines. Healthcare providers can advise on whether a vaccine is safe for you.
